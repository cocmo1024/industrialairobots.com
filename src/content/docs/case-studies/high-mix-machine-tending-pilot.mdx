# High-Mix Machine Tending Pilot

High-mix environments are where machine tending pilots become genuinely informative. A low-variation demo can make almost any tending concept look good. A high-mix pilot forces the team to confront changeovers, fixture realities, operator intervention, and whether the cell can survive beyond a single scripted part family.

## What this pilot pattern is trying to prove

This kind of pilot usually needs to validate:

- whether the target part range is narrow enough for a shared cell concept;
- whether fixturing and gripper logic can survive variation;
- how much human support is still required during normal changeover;
- whether the throughput gains remain credible outside a best-case demo.

Those answers matter more than whether the robot can complete a single polished tending loop.

## Hidden failure points

High-mix pilots often expose problems that simple pilots hide:

- gripper and fixture assumptions that only fit one part family;
- too much setup time between runs;
- exception handling that depends on a specialist being nearby;
- ROI models that ignore engineering effort to maintain the expanded part mix.

That is why many good teams deliberately use a high-mix pilot to decide where the automation boundary should stop.

## Why the outcome matters

If the pilot succeeds, the manufacturer gains a stronger basis for:

- targeting families of parts rather than every possible job;
- deciding whether a cobot or six-axis path is more scalable;
- standardizing fixturing and staging rules before larger rollout;
- estimating what real staffing and maintenance support will be required.

If the pilot struggles, that is still useful. It may show that the process needs better presentation discipline before more robotics investment makes sense.
